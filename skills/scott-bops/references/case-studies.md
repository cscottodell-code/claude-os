# Background Ops Case Studies

Real stories that illustrate the principles in action. Reference these when coaching
to make abstract frameworks concrete. People remember stories better than rules.

---

## Sam Carpenter's TAS Business
**Framework illustrated:** Background Operations (EIB Protocol), Value Stream Mapping

Sam Carpenter was working 100-hour weeks in his telephone-answering business,
constantly firefighting errors. The breaking point: a $3,000 deposit was lost
under a manager's car seat.

**What he did:**
1. **Explicated** the chaotic deposit process into 53 written steps
2. **Improved** by identifying waste and streamlining
3. **Backgroundized** with a check-reader scanner

**Result:** Reduced the process from 53 steps to 23, completely removing himself
from the task. Saved 104 hours per year on this one process alone.

**The lesson:** You cannot improve what you haven't made explicit. The chaos felt
unsolvable until every step was written down. Once visible, the waste was obvious.

## Ray Dalio's Error Log at Bridgewater
**Framework illustrated:** Universal Principles, Issue/Error Log

In the 1990s, a Bridgewater trader forgot to execute a trade, costing the fund
hundreds of thousands of dollars. The natural response would be to fire the trader.

**What Dalio did instead:**
1. **Diagnosed** the universal principle: "Mistakes happen" is a category, not an incident
2. **Codified** the rule: If you log a mistake, you are safe. If you hide it, you are in deep trouble.
3. **Operationalized** the Error Log as a permanent institutional tool
4. **Entrained** the culture until logging errors was automatic

**Result:** The trading machine continually improved because errors surfaced
transparently instead of being hidden. The system got better over time instead
of repeating the same failures.

**The lesson:** Punishing individual mistakes causes cover-ups. Making error
logging safe turns every mistake into a system improvement. The error isn't
the person - it's the machine.

## Carlos Miceli's Daily Skype Calls
**Framework illustrated:** Explore/Exploit, Operational Entrainment

Living in Argentina with no network and poor English, Carlos Miceli recognized
he needed a system for building connections - not motivation or willpower.

**What he did:**
1. **Identified the need:** Exploration (building network from scratch)
2. **Codified the behavior:** One Skype call every single day with an interesting person globally
3. **Made it a Hard Rule:** Not optional, not "when I feel like it" - daily
4. **Entrained:** Repeated until it was automatic

**Result:** Built hundreds of connections and native English fluency without
relying on motivation. The system ran whether he felt like it or not.

**The lesson:** Exploration can be systematized just like exploitation. You don't
need to "feel inspired" to network - you need a rule and a schedule.

## The Battle of Utica (203 BC)
**Framework illustrated:** Operational Entrainment, Failure of Consistency

The Carthaginians knew the best practices for camp construction and sentry duty.
They had the knowledge. But sentry duty is boring, and >99% of the time nothing
happens.

**What went wrong:**
- Guards grew lax because the routine felt pointless
- Camp defenses weren't maintained to standard
- Known procedures were skipped because "nothing ever happens"

**What happened:**
The Roman general Scipio exploited this failure of operational consistency to
launch a night fire attack. 30,000 men were destroyed. The tide of Western
civilization turned on the failure to follow boring, routine best practices.

**The lesson:** The most dangerous failure mode is neglecting routine operations
precisely because they're boring and "nothing usually goes wrong." Entrainment
exists to make boring-but-critical behaviors automatic so they survive human
laziness and complacency. The cost of consistency is low. The cost of a single
lapse can be catastrophic.

## When to Reference These Stories

| Situation | Tell this story |
|-----------|----------------|
| Scott is reluctant to write down a process ("I know how it works") | Sam Carpenter - you can't improve what you haven't explicated |
| Scott wants to punish a mistake or fire someone over an error | Ray Dalio - make logging safe, improve the machine |
| Scott says "I'll network when I feel like it" or lacks motivation | Carlos Miceli - systematize exploration, don't rely on feelings |
| Scott is skipping routine maintenance or "boring" tasks | Battle of Utica - the boring stuff is what saves you |
