## What Is EOS

EOS is a holistic, self-sustaining operating system for businesses generating $2M-$50M in revenue with 10-250 employees. Created by Gino Wickman from 20+ years and 1,300+ full-day sessions with 120+ companies. It replaces the patchwork of disconnected ideas, conflicting philosophies, and half-implemented programs that most entrepreneurs use.

EOS is not theory. It is a complete system where every tool connects to every other tool. Core values drive hiring (People). Hiring determines who executes processes (Process). Processes generate tracked data (Data). Data reveals issues to solve (Issues). Issue resolution keeps you on track toward your vision (Vision). Vision creates the accountability that drives traction (Traction). The circle is intentional.

## The Six Key Components

Every business weakness falls into one of these six categories. No exceptions. Strengthening one strengthens the others. Neglecting one weakens the whole system.

1. **Vision** - Getting everyone 100% on the same page with where you are going and how you plan to get there
2. **People** - Surrounding yourself with great people (right people in the right seats)
3. **Data** - Running your business on facts and figures rather than feelings, personalities, opinions, and egos
4. **Issues** - Strengthening your ability to identify, discuss, and solve problems permanently
5. **Process** - Systemizing your business by documenting core processes and getting everyone to follow them
6. **Traction** - Bringing discipline and accountability to execute the vision every day

## Vision Component

The Vision Component answers the question: Where are we going and how do we get there? Most leadership teams assume they are aligned. They are not. The V/TO (Vision/Traction Organizer) forces alignment by answering 8 questions on a 2-page document.

### Question 1: Core Values (3-7)

Timeless guiding principles that define your culture. Not aspirations of what you want to be, but the rules you already play by. Used to hire, fire, review, reward, and recognize.

**How to discover them:**
1. Think of 3 people in the organization you would clone
2. List their defining characteristics
3. Narrow to the truly core principles (3-7)
4. Let the list simmer for 30 days before finalizing

**Examples:**
- McKinley property management uses "Gumby" as a core value and gives employees a Gumby doll to remind them to be flexible, helpful, and optimistic
- "Be Humble, Grow or Die, Help First, Do the Right Thing, Do What You Say"

**Key insight:** When clearly defined, core values attract like-minded people and weed out those who do not fit. People who do not share your values are not "bad" people - they are simply not the right fit for your culture.

### Question 2: Core Focus (Purpose + Niche)

Your organization's sweet spot. Two parts:
- **Purpose/Cause/Passion:** Why the organization exists. 3-7 words, from the heart, not about money.
- **Our Niche:** What your organization does better than anyone else.

Acts as a filter for all future decisions. Prevents getting distracted by "shiny stuff."

**Example:** Asphalt Specialists defined passion as "Winning" and niche as "Quality asphalt paving." Broder & Sachse strayed into powder-coating, lost $300K, and now labels any bad distraction as a "CCT" (the name of the failed venture).

### Question 3: 10-Year Target (BHAG)

One overriding long-range goal. Larger-than-life, magnetizes the team.

**Examples:** ZenaComp set "$10M revenue with 15% net income." Atlas Oil targeted "5 billion gallons moved."

**Implementation:** Ask the leadership team where they see revenue and size in 10 years. Discuss and debate until everyone is excited and aligned.

### Question 4: Marketing Strategy (4 Parts)

**Target Market ("The List"):** Demographic, geographic, and psychographic characteristics of your ideal customer. Example: Image One defined "IT directors in companies with 25+ laser printers in Michigan and Ohio."

**Three Uniques:** Three qualities that make you stand out. Competitors might share one or two, but no one else offers all three. Example: Southwest Airlines = low fares + on-time flights + having fun.

**Proven Process:** A one-page visual showing 3-7 major steps of how you deliver your product/service. Example: Schechter Wealth Strategies uses 6 steps from "Discovery" to "Review and Service."

**Guarantee:** A pledge that pinpoints an industry-wide problem and solves it. Example: Domino's "30 minutes or it's free." Image One "4 hours or it's free."

### Question 5: 3-Year Picture

Vivid image of the company 3 years from now. Choose a specific future date. Agree on targeted revenue, profit, and 1-2 key measurables. Add 10-20 bullet points (number of people, resources, office, product mix).

**Example:** Zoup! tracked "number of stores" (target: 94). Atlas Oil tracked "gallons" (target: 1 billion).

### Question 6: 1-Year Plan

Brings long-range vision down to ground level. Set future date, revenue, profit, and scope measurables. Then 3-7 specific, measurable, attainable goals. Less is more.

**Bad:** "Improve customer satisfaction" (too vague)
**Good:** "Increase average customer rating to a 9" (measurable, black-and-white)

### Question 7: Quarterly Rocks

3-7 absolute most important priorities for the next 90 days. Named after Stephen Covey's analogy: put the big rocks in the glass first, then sand and water fill around them. If you put the sand in first, the big rocks will not fit.

**Process:** Every 90 days, the leadership team lists everything, debates, and narrows to 3-7. Each Rock is owned by one person. Must be specific, measurable, and attainable. After company Rocks are set, individual team members set their own.

**Good Rocks:** "Close $1M in new business." "Hire a new controller."

### Question 8: Issues List

All obstacles, concerns, and opportunities that could prevent reaching the vision. Getting issues out of heads and onto paper is the critical first step. Acts as a long-term parking lot for company-wide issues beyond the current 90 days.

## People Component

**Formula:** Right People (core values) + Right Seats (GWC) = Great Team

### The People Analyzer

Rate each person on each core value using three ratings:
- **+** = exhibits the value most of the time
- **+/-** = sometimes exhibits it, sometimes does not
- **-** = does not exhibit it most of the time

**The Bar:** Recommended minimum for 5 core values = 3 pluses, 2 plus/minuses, never a minus. Leadership team must agree on the bar.

**Three-Strike Rule:**
- Strike 1: Discuss issues and expectations. 30 days to correct.
- Strike 2: Discuss performance again. Another 30 days.
- Strike 3: No improvement. They must go.

In practice, you rarely need to fire people. Once core values are reinforced through speeches, meetings, and the People Analyzer, people who do not fit often leave on their own.

**Rolling out the People Analyzer (4 Steps):**
1. Analyze each other first (leadership team on themselves)
2. Analyze the entire organization (managers run it on their people)
3. Use it in quarterly reviews (let people analyze you too)
4. Use it to diagnose personnel problems

### The Accountability Chart

Not a traditional org chart. Defines roles, responsibilities, and accountability at every level.

**Three major functions** (every business, regardless of size):
1. Sales and Marketing
2. Operations
3. Finance and Administration

All three must be strong:
- Strong sales + weak ops = bring in customers and lose them out the back door
- Strong sales + strong ops + weak finance = money flows in and right out
- Weak sales + strong ops + strong finance = talented people sitting around waiting

**Structure:**
- Visionary (top, dotted line - not every company needs this)
- Integrator (below Visionary, above the 3 functions)
- 3-7 major functions on the front line
- Each function defined by 5 major roles
- **One name per seat** (one person can hold two seats, never two people in one seat)

**LMA (Lead, Manage, Accountable):** Anyone with people reporting to them has this responsibility. It requires time, energy, and unique ability. Not an afterthought.

**Ground rules for building the chart:**
1. Look forward (6-12 months), not back
2. Detach from current roles and ego - design structure first, then fill names
3. Elevate above the business - make decisions for the long-term greater good

### GWC - The Right-Seat Filter

Strictly binary (yes/no on all three, no gray area):

- **Get it:** Truly understands the role, culture, systems, pace, and how it fits together
- **Want it:** Genuinely likes the job based on fair compensation and responsibility
- **Capacity to do it:** Has the time, mental ability, physical ability, knowledge, skill, and emotional intelligence

**Combined view:**

| Situation | Diagnosis | Action |
|-----------|-----------|--------|
| Passes People Analyzer + GWC | Right person, right seat | Keep and develop |
| Passes People Analyzer, fails GWC | Right person, wrong seat | Find the right seat for them |
| Fails People Analyzer, passes GWC | Wrong person, right seat | Must go (most dangerous) |
| Fails both | Wrong person, wrong seat | Must go immediately |

### Right Person, Wrong Seat vs. Wrong Person, Right Seat

**Right Person, Wrong Seat:** Great cultural fit, but promoted too high, outgrown a seat, or in the wrong role. Find them the right seat. If none exists, you must let them go.

*Home services example:* Best installation tech promoted to ops manager. Team loves him, but he cannot handle scheduling, managing people, and budgets. Move him to lead tech or training role.

**Wrong Person, Right Seat:** Excels at the job but does not share core values. In the short term, results are there. Long term, they chip away at everything you are building. They must go.

*Home services example:* Top salesperson closes more deals than anyone but shades the truth, overpromises, and treats operations poorly.

### The 36 Hours of Pain

When you know someone needs to go but you are procrastinating: the hours leading up to and including the termination will be painful. After about 36 hours, you will realize it was one of the best decisions you ever made. A year of pain vs. 36 hours. The math is clear.

### Delegate and Elevate

Each person has 100% of their working time. If demands require 120%, something has to give. Delegate tasks not in your area of strength so you can elevate to what you do best.

**Stat:** 80% of the time, there is a change on the leadership team within the first 2 years of EOS. Half the time removing someone, half adding someone.

## Data Component

### The Scorecard

5-15 weekly numbers that give you an absolute pulse on the business.

**The Desert Island Exercise:** Imagine you are stranded with no communication except one sheet of paper slipped under your door each week. What 5-15 numbers must be on that paper?

**Building the Scorecard:**
1. Desert Island exercise - identify the vital signs
2. Assign accountability - one person per number
3. Set weekly goals - tied to the 1-year plan
4. Date tracking - upcoming week's date in first column
5. Data collection - decide who physically fills it out

**7 Scorecard Truths:**
1. What gets measured gets done
2. Managing metrics saves time
3. A Scorecard gives you a pulse and ability to predict
4. Inspect what you expect
5. Accountability can exist in a high-trust culture
6. It requires hard work but is worth it
7. One person must own it

**6 Scorecard Fundamentals:**
1. Reviewed in the Weekly L10 Meeting
2. Contains 5-15 numbers
3. Each has an accountable owner
4. Each has a weekly goal
5. Off-track goals drop to Issues List
6. Displays 13 weeks at a glance

### Activity-Based vs. Results-Based Numbers

A P&L is a trailing indicator - it tells you what already happened. The Scorecard tracks leading indicators so you can predict and change the future.

**Sales example:** Do not just track revenue (trailing). Track the sales process backward: leads generated, contacts made, appointments scheduled, proposals sent. If you know your ratios, tracking leads today predicts closes 2-4 months out.

**Customer satisfaction example:** Do not just track lost customers (trailing). Survey customers immediately after service. If average score drops from 8.5 to 7 for three consecutive weeks, you have a leading indicator.

### Everyone Has a Number

Complete mastery = every single employee has a meaningful, manageable number. Even a receptionist ("2 rings good, 3 rings bad").

To find numbers: review the Accountability Chart and the 5 major roles for each seat. Usually 1-3 roles can be translated into a measurable metric.

**8 Advantages of Everyone Having a Number:**
1. Cuts through murky communication
2. Creates accountability
3. Accountable people appreciate numbers
4. Creates clarity and commitment
5. Creates competition
6. Produces results
7. Creates teamwork
8. Solves problems faster

## Issues Component

### Three Types of Issues Lists

1. **V/TO Issues List** - Long-term parking lot for company-wide issues beyond 90 days
2. **Weekly Leadership Team Issues List** - Strategic issues for the current week/quarter (off-track Rocks, bad Scorecard numbers, key employee issues)
3. **Departmental Issues List** - Tactical, day-to-day issues for departmental meetings

### The IDS Track (Identify, Discuss, Solve)

Before starting: review the Issues List and select the top 3 most important. Do not start at the top of the list.

**Step 1: Identify.** The stated problem is rarely the real issue. Dig down, peel the onion, find the root cause. Determine if it is: a problem to solve, information to communicate, or an idea needing feedback.

**Step 2: Discuss.** Everyone shares data, solutions, and concerns openly. Watch for tangents - if the conversation drifts, call out "Tangent Alert!" If the tangent is a real but separate issue, add it to the Issues List.

**Step 3: Solve.** Conclude with a resolution: an action item for the To-Do List, an awareness that everyone concurs with, or a recognition that more research is needed.

### The 10 Commandments of Solving Issues

1. Thou shalt not rule by consensus (Integrator breaks ties)
2. Thou shalt not be a weenie (have firm resolve)
3. Thou shalt be decisive (deciding is more important than what you decide)
4. Thou shalt not rely on secondhand information (pull everyone into a pow-wow)
5. Thou shalt live with it, end it, or change it (no other options)
6. Thou shalt choose short-term pain and suffering (solve now, not later)
7. Thou shalt enter the danger (the scariest issue is the most important one)
8. Thou shalt take a shot (propose a solution, do not wait for someone else)

## Process Component

### The Three-Step Process Documenter

**Step 1: Identify Your Core Processes.** Leadership team agrees on what they are and names them. Typical list: HR process, marketing process, sales process, operations processes (1-3), accounting process, customer-retention process.

**Step 2: Document (The 20/80 Rule).** The person accountable for each function documents it. Only document the high-level 20% that produces 80% of the results. Use checklists. Avoid documenting 100% of every tiny detail (overkill kills adoption).

**Step 3: Package It.** Compile into a single binder or intranet. Title it "[Company Name] Way" (e.g., "The Advosy Way").

### Followed By All (FBA)

Getting processes followed requires:
1. Show employees how the system creates efficiencies that make their lives easier
2. Create a visual model like a "Circle of Life" showing how following each step positively affects others, and skipping a step negatively impacts the whole
3. Train everyone
4. Actively manage adherence

## Traction Component

### Rocks (90-Day Priorities)

Humans naturally lose focus after ~90 days. The "90-Day World" resets priorities quarterly.

**Setting Rocks:**
1. Leadership team lists everything that needs doing
2. Debate and narrow to 3-7 company Rocks
3. Each must be specific, measurable, and attainable
4. Each owned by one person
5. After company Rocks, individuals set their own Rocks

**Criteria:** At the end of 90 days, it must be black-and-white whether the Rock was completed. No ambiguity.

### The Meeting Pulse

**Annual Planning:** Two days off-site. Review and update the V/TO. Set next year's goals and Q1 Rocks.

**Quarterly Planning:** One day off-site. Review quarterly progress. Set next quarter's Rocks.

**Weekly Level 10 Meeting:** 90 minutes, same day/time every week. The core heartbeat.

**L10 Agenda:**

| Segment | Time | Purpose |
|---------|------|---------|
| Segue | 5 min | Good news. Transition from working IN to working ON the business |
| Scorecard | 5 min | Review 5-15 numbers. Off-track drops to IDS. No discussion here. |
| Rock Review | 5 min | On track / Off track. Off-track drops to IDS. |
| Customer/Employee Headlines | 5 min | Brief good or bad news |
| To-Do List | 5 min | Done / Not done on 7-day items. Target 90%+ completion rate. |
| **IDS** | **60 min** | Priority-ordered issue solving. This is where the real work happens. |
| Conclude | 5 min | Recap new To-Dos, cascading messages, rate meeting 1-10 (target 8+) |

**Key discipline:** Same day, same time, same agenda, start on time, end on time. No exceptions. The rhythm creates accountability.

### Same Page Meeting (V/I only)

Monthly, 2-4 hours, off-site, strictly between the Visionary and Integrator. Agenda includes personal check-in, building an issues list, and IDS. Must not end until both are 100% aligned.

## Visionary/Integrator Relationship

### What Is a Visionary?

The person who provides the creative spark, big ideas, and relentless energy. ~22% of the population has Visionary tendencies.

**What they bring:** Idea generation (~20/week), big picture thinking, future-seeing, hunter mentality, spark plug energy, inspiration, big deal closing, vision creation.

**Visionary's 5 roles on the Accountability Chart:**
1. New Ideas / R&D
2. Creative Problem Solving
3. Major External Relationships
4. Culture
5. Selling Big Deals

**Visionary challenges:** Staying focused (shiny objects), too many ideas (organizational whiplash), hates details, poor at developing talent, under-communicates the vision, inconsistent priorities, ADD-like behavior.

### What Is an Integrator?

The person who harmoniously integrates major functions, runs day-to-day operations, and translates big ideas into executable plans. Only ~5.5% of the population is cut out for this role.

**What they produce:** Clarity, communication, resolution, focus, accountability, team unity, follow-through, execution.

**Integrator's 5 roles on the Accountability Chart:**
1. LMA (Lead, Manage, Accountable)
2. Executing the Business Plan / P&L Results
3. Integrating Major Functions
4. Resolving Cross-Functional Issues
5. Communication Across the Organization

**Integrator challenges:** No glory / thankless job, labeled as pessimist, bad guy role, discipline burden, perceived as slowing things down, loneliness, not Superman.

**8-Point Job Description:**
1. Faithfully executes the business plan (P&L objectives)
2. Leads, manages, holds leadership team accountable
3. Integrates all major operating functions
4. Resolves issues effectively (comfortable with conflict)
5. Ensures adherence to core processes and operating system
6. Demonstrates relentless obsession with values alignment, focus, simplicity, clarity
7. Collaborates effectively with the Visionary, stays on the same page
8. Confirms key messages are properly cascaded

**Key insight:** Industry experience is optional. James Couzens knew nothing about automobiles. The best HVAC technician is not necessarily the right Integrator. The role depends on managing human energy, creating structure, and executing plans.

### The 5 Rules for the V/I Relationship

**Rule 1: Stay on the Same Page.** Monthly Same Page Meeting: 2-4 hours, off-site, just V and I. Personal check-in, build issues list, IDS until 100% aligned. Meeting does not end until both are aligned.

**Rule 2: No End Runs.** Employees will try to bypass the Integrator and go to the Visionary to complain or get a different answer. Visionaries must shut this down: "Are you going to tell 'em, or am I going to tell 'em?"

**Rule 3: Integrator is the Tie Breaker.** On day-to-day operational decisions, when the team is divided, the Integrator breaks the tie. Consensus management is a recipe for failure.

**Rule 4: You Are an Employee When Working "in" the Business.** Visionaries separate their Owner's Box (profit distributions, high-level strategy) from their Employee seat (follow company rules, can technically be fired for non-performance).

**Rule 5: Maintain Mutual Respect.** Business marriage. Never speak negatively about each other to anyone in the organization. Genuinely respect each other's distinct talents.

### Productive Tension

V and I are Yin and Yang. Their polarity creates an electric charge. When properly harnessed, this creative tension produces better questions, superior solutions, and exponential expansion of force. This is "Rocket Fuel."

### The Crystallization Process (7 Steps)

1. **Use the Visionary Spectrum** - Assess how much Visionary energy your company needs (industry type, growth aspirations, market complexity)
2. **Map your Visionary profile** - Honestly assess your traits, strengths, weaknesses
3. **Identify your ideal Integrator profile** - Design the complementary puzzle piece
4. **Use the Four Readiness Factors** - Financial, Psychological, Lifestyle, Unique Ability readiness
5. **Initiate the search** - Networking, recruiters, in-house candidates, or fractional Integrators
6. **On-board and ramp-up** - First 90 days: accelerate learning, secure early wins, build trust
7. **Follow the V/I Relationship Cycle** - Plan, Execute, Same Page, Align, Repeat

### Four Readiness Factors

1. **Financial Readiness** - Can you afford it? Take your compensation number and add 50%.
2. **Psychological Readiness** - Are you ready to let go of some control? (Hardest factor)
3. **Lifestyle Readiness** - Ready for fewer hours, or same hours with different focus?
4. **Unique Ability Readiness** - Ready to operate 100% in your zone of genius?

### Common Failure Modes

- **Not Letting Go** - Visionary maintains direct control over departments, demands dotted-line reporting that undermines Integrator
- **Multiple Seats Without Compartmentalizing** - Failing to clarify which hat you are wearing during decisions
- **Rushing the 1-Year Milestone** - Takes a full year for V/I to hit stride. "Visionary Remorse" where the Visionary feels put out to pasture
- **False Starts** - Hiring an Integrator but never giving them autonomy

### Famous V/I Pairs

| Visionary | Integrator | Company | Key Insight |
|-----------|-----------|---------|-------------|
| Ray Kroc | Fred Turner | McDonald's | Turner built the operations manual and franchise consistency |
| Walt Disney | Roy Disney | Disney | Roy raised money and kept company solvent through Walt's ambitions |
| Soichiro Honda | Takeo Fujisawa | Honda | 25-year partnership. Honda was the "flaming Visionary," Fujisawa brought discipline |
| Henry Ford | James Couzens | Ford | 12-year partnership. Couzens managed finances, sales network, org structure |

## Organizational Checkup

20 statements rated 1 (weak) to 5 (strong) by each leadership team member. Covers all six components.

**Scoring:**
- 20-34%: Serious problems, likely in crisis
- 35-49%: Below average, significant work needed
- 50-64%: Average, some things working but major gaps
- 65-79%: Above average, good foundation with room to improve
- 80-100%: Strong, operating at a high level

Most companies score 20-60% on first assessment. Repeat quarterly to track progress. The most valuable part is not the score but where leadership team members disagree on ratings. That is where the real issues live.

## Five Leadership Abilities

Skills that help break through growth ceilings:

1. **Simplify** - Push against the natural tendency to add complexity. Fewer services, simpler processes, clearer message.
2. **Delegate** - Give people full ownership of outcomes, not just tasks. The skill that started the company is now the skill holding it back.
3. **Predict** - Short-term (next 90 days based on pipeline and capacity). Long-term (3, 5, 10 years). Get ahead of problems.
4. **Systemize** - Document and standardize core processes. The difference between consistent quality and "whatever feels right that day."
5. **Structure** - Organize the company to support growth. What works for 15 people will not work for 50.

## Four Fundamental Beliefs

1. **Build and maintain a true leadership team** - 3-7 people genuinely committed, willing to be vulnerable, able to engage in healthy debate
2. **Hitting the ceiling is inevitable** - Happens at predictable revenue levels ($1M, $3M, $5M, $10M). Each requires you to change.
3. **Operate on one operating system** - No cherry-picking. All in or not at all.
4. **Be open-minded, growth-oriented, and vulnerable** - Vulnerability is strength, not weakness.

---

## Five Common Frustrations

1. **Lack of Control** - The business runs you instead of the other way around
2. **People** - Cannot find, hire, or keep good people
3. **Profit** - Revenue growing but money evaporates
4. **The Ceiling** - Growth stalled at an invisible barrier
5. **Nothing Is Working** - Tried everything, nothing produces lasting results

These are not character flaws. They are symptoms of a business that has outgrown its current operating system.

---

## Letting Go of the Vine

An entrepreneur is like a person swinging through the jungle on a vine. To keep moving, you must let go of the current vine (your habits, control, "I'll do it myself" mentality) and grab the next one (trusting your team, building systems, stepping out of day-to-day). For a moment, you are in free fall. That moment is where most entrepreneurs freeze.

The central challenge of EOS is not learning new concepts. It is the entrepreneur's willingness to change.

For a home services company owner: let your operations manager actually run operations. Trust your sales team to quote jobs. Step out of daily crew management so you can focus on the business instead of working in it.
