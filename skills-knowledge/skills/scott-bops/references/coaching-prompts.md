Read this file when coaching Scott through a specific type of decision or review.
Each section contains the exact questions to ask, in order, with guidance on
how to interpret the answers.

## I. Opportunity Evaluation

**When to use:** Scott is considering a new client, project, commitment, or opportunity.

### The Prompts (ask in order)

1. **The Admiration Test:**
   "If you worked with this person intensely 10+ hours per day for 14+ days straight,
   and you were taking 3 days off to relax on the beach, would you want them to come
   with you rather than being alone?"
   - Yes → Passes. Move to next test.
   - No or hesitation → Reject. Stop here.

2. **Hell Yeah or No:**
   "When you think about this opportunity, do you feel 'Wow, that would be amazing,
   absolutely, hell yeah!' or anything less than that?"
   - Hell yeah → Accept.
   - Anything less → Reject.

### Why these work
Thinking is expensive. Hard rules make a single first cut before you need to weigh
all the variables. They save cognitive capacity for decisions that truly require judgment.

### Follow-up if Scott pushes back
If Scott wants to override the test, ask: "What specific evidence makes this an
exception to the rule?" The burden of proof is on the exception, not the rule.

## II. Habit Calibration

**When to use:** Weekly review, or when Scott mentions habits are slipping or feeling too easy.

### The Prompts (ask in order)

1. **"What was your success rate on tracked habits this week?"**
   - If no tracking in place: "Let's set up a simple daily tracker first. What 3-5 things matter most?"

2. **If >70%:** "How can you increase the difficulty or add new things?"
   - Examples: increase meditation 5→10 min, add a new habit, raise the bar on an existing one

3. **If <70% for two consecutive weeks:** "How can you make things easier to rebuild momentum?"
   - Examples: "run 5 miles" → "lace up shoes and go outside"
   - Remove the least important tracked item
   - Reduce frequency or duration

4. **If 100%:** "The system is too easy. You're stagnating. What's the next level?"

### Calibration Principles
- 70% is the sweet spot: succeeding more often than not while constantly growing
- The system must survive the worst week (5-10 min max daily)
- Binary tracking only (Yes/No, not nuanced scoring)
- Never aim for perfection; use 70% as a mechanical trigger

## III. Process Audit

**When to use:** Before building an automation, designing a workflow, or when a process
feels inefficient.

### The Prompts (ask in order)

1. **"Can you walk me through every step of this process as it exists today?"**
   - Get every step on paper. Every handoff, wait, approval, movement.
   - If Scott is vague: "What happens between step X and step Y? Walk me through the details."

2. **"At what points do you make decisions that directly affect the final product?"**
   - Mark these as value-producing steps.

3. **"When do you actually conduct important tests or analysis that impacts those decisions?"**
   - Also value-producing.

4. **"Which of these steps actually end up shaping the final product?"**
   - Sum up the value-producing steps.

5. **"Are any of these steps just waiting, correcting, moving, or processing?"**
   - If yes → this is waste. Target for elimination.

6. **"What would this look like with zero waste?"**
   - This is the target state for EIB.

### After the audit
Walk through EIB: "Now that we can see the waste, let's improve before we backgroundize.
Which waste steps can we eliminate entirely? Which can we combine? What's left to automate?"

## IV. Problem Diagnosis

**When to use:** A mistake occurred, a recurring problem surfaced, or there's systemic
friction somewhere.

### The Prompts (ask in order)

1. **"What is the 'what is'?"**
   - Focus on objectively identifying current reality before proposing any solution.
   - If Scott jumps to solutions: "Hold on. Let's spend a few more minutes on the diagnosis."

2. **"Is this 'another one of those'?"**
   - Identify the broader category: hiring, compensation, communication, process, tooling, etc.

3. **"What is the universal root cause?"**
   - Not the proximate cause (what happened), but the systemic cause (why it keeps happening).

4. **"What universal rule or action statement can we codify to handle all future decisions of this type?"**
   - The rule should handle the entire category, not just this instance.

5. **"How do we operationalize this?"**
   - Build tools, dashboards, processes, or Hard Rules to enforce the principle.

### Critical timing
Diagnosis must take 15-60 minutes and happen BEFORE prescription. If Scott wants to
jump to fixing: "I know it's tempting to fix this now, but spending 15 minutes on
root cause will prevent this entire class of problem from recurring."

## V. Explore vs. Exploit

**When to use:** Scott seems stuck between focusing on current work and exploring
new directions. Also when discussing bad habits or feeling restless.

### The Prompts

1. **"Are you regularly experiencing significant boredom or fatigue?"**
   - Yes → This is a biological signal to shift from exploiting to exploring.
   - No → Stay in exploit mode. Ride the productive wave.

2. **If in a bad habit loop:** "What underlying needs is this activity meeting?"
   - Identify: mastery, rapid rewards, escape, social connection, etc.

3. **"Where will the sense of mastery and rapid rewards come from if you quit this behavior?"**
   - The replacement must provide a similar feedback loop or the quit won't stick.

### Mode Assessment
- **Signs of healthy exploitation:** Energy, momentum, clear priorities, visible progress
- **Signs you need to explore:** Boredom, diminishing returns, restlessness, going through motions
- **Signs of unhealthy exploitation:** Burnout, >60% capacity sustained, neglecting other domains

### Daily rhythm reminder
Morning = exploit (decisive action), Evening = explore (learning, networking, new things).
Don't fight the natural energy cycle.

## VI. Crisis Response

**When to use:** Scott is under severe stress, facing ambiguity, or in a
catastrophic failure situation. Rational thinking may be shutting down.

### The Prompt

Repeat this exact sequence:
**"What do you have to get done? What do you have to get done? What do you have to get done?"**

### Why this specific phrasing
The more thoughts spent on fear, the less working memory is available for effective action.
This entrained question interrupts fixation and forces the brain back into rational execution.
The repetition is intentional - it breaks the panic loop.

### Follow-up
Once Scott names 1-3 concrete things:
"Good. Just those. Nothing else right now. Which one first?"

## VII. Creative Block

**When to use:** Scott is stuck while writing, building, designing, or creating anything.

### The Prompt

**"Okay, what are you trying to say here?"**

### Why this works
Writer's block occurs because planning and translating are competing for the same
working memory. This question forces a micro-cycle:
1. Stop creating (release the translation burden)
2. Brainstorm (what ideas am I working with?)
3. Categorize (how do they group?)
4. Outline (what's the sequence?)
5. Resume creating (with working memory freed up)

### If the block persists
- "Let's go back further. What are all the ideas you have, even half-baked ones?"
  (Brainstorm phase)
- "Now group those. What themes do you see?" (Categorize phase)
- "What order makes sense?" (Outline phase)
- "Now write. Don't edit until the first draft is done." (Create phase)
