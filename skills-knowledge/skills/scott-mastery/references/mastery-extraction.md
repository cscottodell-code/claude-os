## Full Profiles (Top 20)

### 1. The Seven Deadly Realities (Phase IV, General Knowledge)
- **Principle:** Human nature includes universal negative traits like envy, conformism, rigidity, self-obsessiveness, laziness, flightiness, and passive aggression. Navigating the workplace effectively requires recognizing these realities rather than projecting your own emotional needs or idealized expectations onto others.
- **Recognition Pattern:** A colleague suddenly takes credit for your work (laziness), outright dismisses a new software tool without reviewing it (rigidity), or offers excessive, unearned praise upfront before subtly undermining you (envy).
- **Defensive Play:** Accept these traits as permanent, predictable fixtures of the human comedy rather than getting emotional or taking them personally. Keep your ideas guarded from lazy peers and downplay your own talents around insecure, envious ones.
- **Offensive Play:** Frame all your requests by appealing directly to the other person's self-interest. Use their predictable, ego-driven behaviors to maneuver them into supporting your sales strategies or software adoptions without triggering their resistance.
- **Red Flags:** Colleagues who agree to your face but chronically delay action (passive aggression), or sudden mood swings dictating their support for a project (flightiness).
- **Daily Check-in:** Did I mistake someone's friendly mask for genuine support today, or fail to frame my request around their actual self-interest?

### 2. Craft the Appropriate Persona (Phase IV, Strategy 2)
- **Principle:** People will judge you based on your outward appearance and naturally project their own assumptions and fantasies onto you. Rather than simply "being yourself," consciously mold your public image to fit the corporate environment, keeping your true self detached and in control.
- **Recognition Pattern:** A peer who always seems perfectly composed and adopts the exact tone needed for every specific audience, blending seamlessly into different executive environments.
- **Defensive Play:** Look past the flawless, accommodating exterior of colleagues to observe their actual actions and decisions, preventing yourself from being blinded by their charm or manufactured authority.
- **Offensive Play:** Adopt a flexible professional mask that highlights your competence while keeping your personal vulnerabilities hidden. Adjust your communication style: be deeply technical with the engineers, then pivot to an aggressive, results-oriented persona for the sales floor.
- **Red Flags:** Finding yourself constantly trying to decipher what a colleague truly thinks, indicating their persona is successfully keeping you guessing and off-balance.
- **Daily Check-in:** Did my outward behavior today strategically advance my goals, or did I let my raw emotions dictate my professional image?

### 3. Deep Observation (Phase II, Step One: The Passive Mode)
- **Principle:** When entering a new environment or launching a new initiative, suppress the urge to immediately impress others or seek attention. Quietly observe the unwritten rules, procedures, and underlying power dynamics to understand how the group truly functions before asserting yourself.
- **Recognition Pattern:** A new executive who stays quiet in their first few cross-departmental meetings, asking careful questions and watching who defers to whom rather than pitching their own ideas.
- **Defensive Play:** Be aware that quiet, observant colleagues are mapping out your weaknesses and the company's power structures; do not reveal all your strategies or frustrations to them.
- **Offensive Play:** Before rolling out a new AI tool, spend time watching how the sales reps actually work and discovering who holds the real social influence on the floor, so you can tailor your rollout to the actual company culture.
- **Red Flags:** Feeling an overwhelming urge to speak up and prove your intelligence in a room where you do not yet fully understand the political landscape.
- **Daily Check-in:** Who actually drove the decisions in today's meeting, and what unwritten rules dictated the conversation?

### 4. Combine the "How" and the "What" (Phase II, Strategy 7)
- **Principle:** Avoid separating the grand vision of a project from the granular mechanics of how it is actually built. By deeply understanding the underlying technology or structure (the "how"), you can innovate and push the boundaries of the final product or service (the "what").
- **Recognition Pattern:** A leader who doesn't just read the high-level sales dashboard, but sits down to understand the exact code, data pipelines, and CRM mechanics feeding into that dashboard.
- **Defensive Play:** When someone presents a flashy, high-level strategy, aggressively question the technical feasibility and structural realities to ensure it is not just an empty surface illusion.
- **Offensive Play:** Use your dual knowledge of sales strategy and full-stack coding to design software that fits perfectly into the reps' actual workflows, bridging the gap between business needs and technical limits.
- **Red Flags:** A project that looks incredible in a slide deck but lacks a clear, functional explanation of how the underlying systems will actually integrate.
- **Daily Check-in:** Did I rely too much on surface-level metrics today without verifying the mechanics generating them?

### 5. Synthesize All Forms of Knowledge (Phase VI, Strategy 7)
- **Principle:** The human brain is naturally designed to make connections, but corporate environments artificially separate knowledge into specialized silos. True mastery comes from breaking down these barriers, blending disciplines, and developing a comprehensive understanding of the entire business ecosystem.
- **Recognition Pattern:** A colleague who consistently draws parallels between completely different departments or industries to solve a localized problem.
- **Defensive Play:** Do not allow highly specialized peers to dismiss your cross-functional ideas just because you lack their narrow pedigree.
- **Offensive Play:** Continuously blend your knowledge of human psychology, sales tactics, and software development to create unified automated systems that a purely technical engineer or a purely traditional salesperson could never conceive.
- **Red Flags:** Teams becoming too comfortable in their own specialized jargon and refusing to collaborate with other business units.
- **Daily Check-in:** What idea from outside my core discipline did I successfully apply to a problem today?

### 6. Create a Back-and-Forth Dynamic (Phase III, Strategy 4)
- **Principle:** The most effective learning and coaching relationships must eventually move past a passive, top-down approach. As competence grows, the dynamic must become interactive, with the pupil providing feedback, adjusting ideas, and pushing back against the teacher.
- **Recognition Pattern:** A coaching session where the trainee actively challenges the sales script, offering their own field-tested modifications.
- **Defensive Play:** If a leader demands rigid, unquestioning obedience to their methods, recognize they are stifling your growth; adapt their ideas quietly to your own style.
- **Offensive Play:** When training sales reps, encourage them to adapt your pitches to their own personalities and bring their field experiences back to you.
- **Red Flags:** A training program or meeting that feels completely one-sided, where participants simply nod and take notes.
- **Daily Check-in:** Did I adapt my approach based on feedback from my team today, or did I just lecture them?

### 7. The Evolutionary Hijack (Phase V, Strategy 7)
- **Principle:** Creativity and product development do not follow a straight line, but are organic processes shaped by adapting to unexpected contingencies. Instead of forcing a rigid plan, you must remain fluid, exploring side paths and letting real-world feedback shape the final outcome.
- **Recognition Pattern:** A software launch that radically pivots its core feature set based on early, unexpected user behavior.
- **Defensive Play:** Avoid tying your reputation to the success of a single, rigid business plan.
- **Offensive Play:** Release automated tools and sales scripts as rapid prototypes, watch carefully how reps actually use them, and immediately exploit those unexpected behaviors to build a better system.
- **Red Flags:** A project team that continues to push a failing strategy simply because it was the original plan.
- **Daily Check-in:** What unexpected piece of data or feedback did I ignore today because it didn't fit my preconceived plan?

### 8. Submit to the Other (Phase VI, Strategy 6: The Inside-out Perspective)
- **Principle:** We naturally project our own beliefs and values onto others, remaining on the outside looking in. To truly understand people or markets, you must overcome your biases and deeply immerse yourself in their specific worldview.
- **Recognition Pattern:** A leader who spends a week shadowing entry-level technicians in the field to genuinely understand their operational pain points.
- **Defensive Play:** When negotiating, realize the other party is likely projecting their own corporate values onto you; use this blind spot to anticipate their next move.
- **Offensive Play:** When building an AI tool for your sales team, discard your executive perspective. Sit on the phones, take rejections, and feel the exact workflow friction the reps feel.
- **Red Flags:** Finding yourself dismissing another department's processes as "stupid" or "backward" without understanding the environmental pressures that created them.
- **Daily Check-in:** Did I judge someone's actions today based on my own assumptions, rather than trying to understand the pressures they were under?

### 9. Advance Through Trial and Error (Phase II, Strategy 8)
- **Principle:** The most powerful way to learn a complex skill is through a hands-on, iterative approach of experimentation. By building things, making mistakes, and immediately seeing the results, you learn faster than through abstract study.
- **Recognition Pattern:** An employee who skips reading the thick software manual and instead immediately starts clicking around, breaking things, and figuring out the system's limits.
- **Defensive Play:** Do not trust a strategy or product built entirely on theoretical market research; demand a functional prototype.
- **Offensive Play:** Teach yourself new coding frameworks by building small, low-stakes applications that solve immediate problems for your sales team.
- **Red Flags:** Feeling paralyzed by the need to understand every theoretical aspect of a subject before taking any tangible action.
- **Daily Check-in:** What small, calculated risk did I take today to test an idea in the real world?

### 10. See Yourself as Others See You (Phase IV, Strategy 3)
- **Principle:** We are often blind to our own social flaws and the ways we alienate others. By looking at past conflicts objectively and detaching from our emotions, we can identify our off-putting behaviors and self-correct.
- **Recognition Pattern:** A colleague who openly solicits harsh feedback after a failed presentation, genuinely adjusting their behavior.
- **Defensive Play:** Notice when peers are unaware of how abrasive they are; leverage their lack of self-awareness by staying calm.
- **Offensive Play:** Review a recent meeting where your ideas were rejected. Instead of blaming their stupidity, analyze your own bluntness that may have caused them to feel threatened.
- **Red Flags:** Repeatedly involved in the same types of disputes while believing you are always the victim.
- **Daily Check-in:** How might my colleagues have interpreted my tone and body language during today's most stressful interaction?

### 11. Suffer Fools Gladly (Phase IV, Strategy 4)
- **Principle:** The corporate world is filled with people driven by ego, short-term vanity, and political drama. Attempting to change them is a waste of energy; accept them as a part of nature and exploit their predictable foolishness.
- **Recognition Pattern:** A manager who calmly nods and smiles while a peer goes on an egotistical rant, completely unbothered, while gathering information.
- **Defensive Play:** Put up a pleasant front with petty colleagues, keep interactions brief, and never lower yourself to fighting them on emotional terms.
- **Offensive Play:** Feed the egos of rigid executives by publicly giving them credit for ideas, using their vanity to secure budget approvals while keeping operational control.
- **Red Flags:** Feeling intense frustration or a desire to "prove them wrong" when dealing with an illogical colleague.
- **Daily Check-in:** Did I let an insecure or foolish colleague drain my emotional energy today?

### 12. Widen Your Vision (Phase VI, Strategy 5: The Global Perspective)
- **Principle:** In competitive environments, those who react emotionally to immediate events lose; those who maintain a wider perspective win. Look past daily setbacks to see how every action connects to your long-term goals.
- **Recognition Pattern:** A leader who does not panic when the team loses a major deal, but uses the loss to adjust the broader annual strategy.
- **Defensive Play:** Notice when competitors are fixating on short-term metrics; use their tunnel vision against them.
- **Offensive Play:** Train your sales reps to view the entire month of calls as a single, fluid campaign where rejections refine the ultimate pitch.
- **Red Flags:** Feeling consumed by the details of a single minor dispute, losing sight of the bigger picture.
- **Daily Check-in:** Did my decisions today serve my overarching, long-term objective, or was I just putting out fires?

### 13. Skills Acquisition (Phase II, Step Two: The Practice Mode)
- **Principle:** Mastering any complex skill requires enduring an initial period of tedium and frustration. By isolating one fundamental task and repeating it intensely, you hardwire the knowledge into your brain.
- **Recognition Pattern:** An employee who volunteers to run the same boring report every day until they can execute it flawlessly.
- **Defensive Play:** Do not trust expertise claims from those who constantly jump from one new trend to another.
- **Offensive Play:** Embrace the boredom of learning backend server architecture by dedicating two hours of intense daily focus exclusively to that task.
- **Red Flags:** Feeling the urge to multitask or jump to an advanced technique before achieving fluency in the basics.
- **Daily Check-in:** Did I push through the frustration of practicing a weak skill today, or did I retreat to tasks that are already easy?

### 14. Mechanical Intelligence (Phase V, Strategy 3)
- **Principle:** True innovation comes from a deep, practical connection to the materials you are working with, rather than relying solely on abstract theories. By building, testing, and experiencing your own designs, you spot structural flaws.
- **Recognition Pattern:** A software developer who insists on acting as the end-user for their own application before releasing it.
- **Defensive Play:** Dismiss strategies crafted entirely by executives who have never directly used the company's product.
- **Offensive Play:** Write the code for an internal sales tool, personally deploy it, and use it to execute live sales calls, directly feeling the friction points.
- **Red Flags:** Designing a tool strictly from a whiteboard without physically handling the systems your reps use.
- **Daily Check-in:** Did I rely on abstract data to evaluate a system today, or did I get my hands dirty?

### 15. Alternate the Mind through "The Current" (Phase V, Step Two C)
- **Principle:** Deep understanding requires an ongoing feedback loop between abstract speculation and real-world observation. Theory without testing loses touch with reality; data without questions fails to innovate.
- **Recognition Pattern:** A team that brainstorms on Monday, builds a prototype by Wednesday, tests it Friday, and revises.
- **Defensive Play:** Challenge colleagues who present endless data without bold conclusions, and those who push grand theories but refuse to test them.
- **Offensive Play:** Develop a hypothesis about why a service package isn't selling, quickly test a new pricing layout on a small subset, and use the data to refine your theory.
- **Red Flags:** Spending months refining a strategic plan in isolation without validation.
- **Daily Check-in:** Did I test any of my assumptions against actual, messy reality today?

### 16. Speak Through Your Work (Phase IV, Strategy 1)
- **Principle:** Engaging in office politics or trying to verbally defend yourself is a waste of energy. The most powerful way to silence detractors is to deliver undeniably excellent results.
- **Recognition Pattern:** An engineer who ignores a lengthy email debate and instead quietly builds a functioning prototype.
- **Defensive Play:** When facing an insecure manager, stop arguing. Quietly build a flawless project that makes them look good.
- **Offensive Play:** Instead of debating peer executives, deploy your AI software to a small pilot group and let the revenue numbers do the talking.
- **Red Flags:** Feeling the compulsion to write a long, angry email defending your professional reputation.
- **Daily Check-in:** Did I waste time arguing about an idea today that I could have simply proven through execution?

### 17. Let Go of the Past (Phase I, Strategy 4: The Adaptation Strategy)
- **Principle:** You must not tie your identity to a specific job title, past success, or outdated technology. As the market shifts, ruthlessly abandon old ways to adapt.
- **Recognition Pattern:** A veteran sales manager who willingly abandons the legacy CRM they mastered to champion a new AI-driven tool.
- **Defensive Play:** Identify colleagues stubbornly defending obsolete processes; work around them.
- **Offensive Play:** Willingly shed your past identity as merely a "spreadsheet expert." Leverage your deep sales psychology knowledge to become the company's leading architect of automated revenue systems.
- **Red Flags:** Catching yourself saying, "But this is the way we've always done it."
- **Daily Check-in:** What outdated assumption or comfortable habit did I cling to today?

### 18. Move Toward Resistance and Pain (Phase II, Strategy 5)
- **Principle:** Our natural tendency is to practice what we're good at. True mastery requires deliberately seeking out tasks that are painful, tedious, and difficult.
- **Recognition Pattern:** A speaker who records their own presentations to critique their nervous tics.
- **Defensive Play:** Recognize that peers who only coast on natural talents will eventually hit a plateau.
- **Offensive Play:** Because you are naturally open and trusting, force yourself into uncomfortable negotiations where you must practice holding your tongue and hiding your intentions.
- **Red Flags:** Designing your workday entirely around tasks you enjoy, consistently delegating the tasks that intimidate you.
- **Daily Check-in:** What uncomfortable task or difficult conversation did I actively seek out today?

### 19. Trust the Process (Phase II, Strategy 4)
- **Principle:** Learning a complex skill involves a steep curve filled with panic and inadequacy. You must suppress the urge to quit and trust that repeated practice will eventually make it fluid.
- **Recognition Pattern:** A junior coder who fails their first three code reviews but keeps asking for harder assignments.
- **Defensive Play:** When implementing a new system, anticipate that your team will hit a wall and want to abandon it; hold the line.
- **Offensive Play:** When overwhelmed by full-stack development, ignore the panic. Break it down to small daily exercises and trust the process.
- **Red Flags:** Interpreting learning frustration as a sign you're "just not built for this."
- **Daily Check-in:** Did I interpret today's frustration as failure, or as the necessary friction of building new neural pathways?

### 20. Transform Yourself Through Practice (Phase VI, Strategy 3: The Fingertip Feel)
- **Principle:** By practicing fundamental mechanics endlessly, they become automatic. This frees the conscious mind to see the entire dynamic in real-time, resulting in instinctual decision-making.
- **Recognition Pattern:** A seasoned negotiator who effortlessly counters objections and reads the room without looking at notes.
- **Defensive Play:** Do not attempt to improvise against an opponent who has a "fingertip feel"; rely on preparation.
- **Offensive Play:** Drill your sales reps on objection-handling scripts until they can recite them backward. Only then will they have the bandwidth to genuinely read customer emotions live.
- **Red Flags:** Having to consciously think about every step of a basic workflow.
- **Daily Check-in:** Am I relying on last-minute improvisation, or have I drilled the fundamentals so deeply that execution is automatic?

## Dangerous Combinations

### 1. The Stealth Saboteur
**Principles**: Craft the Appropriate Persona + Deep Observation + The Seven Deadly Realities (Passive Aggression and Envy)
A colleague acts perfectly composed and friendly while quietly mapping your vulnerabilities. Driven by hidden envy, they use this intelligence to subtly delay your projects.
**Counter**: Focus exclusively on their track record and actions over time, not their friendly exterior.

### 2. The Parasitic Innovator
**Principles**: Deep Observation + The Seven Deadly Realities (Laziness) + Transfigure Their Ideas
A coworker watches you do the hard work of trial-and-error, then swoops in, tweaks your concept slightly, and presents the polished version as their own.
**Counter**: Keep work-in-progress guarded until complete, and establish your credit upfront.

### 3. The Bureaucratic Quagmire
**Principles**: The Seven Deadly Realities (Rigidity) + Suffer Fools Gladly
An entrenched manager feigns support while using rigid procedures to indefinitely stall your initiative.
**Counter**: Outwardly display deference while quietly launching a small prototype that delivers undeniable results.

### 4. The False Mentor
**Principles**: The Mentor Dynamic + Craft the Appropriate Persona + The Seven Deadly Realities (Self-obsessiveness and Envy)
A senior leader presents themselves as your champion but actually keeps you doing their heavy lifting and blocks your exposure.
**Counter**: Maintain emotional distance and quietly expand your network outside their silo.

### 5. The Shape-Shifter
**Principles**: Submit to the Other + Craft the Appropriate Persona + The Seven Deadly Realities (Conformism)
A peer figures out what the CEO wants to hear, molds their image to match, and gains rapid promotions through conformity rather than competence.
**Counter**: Silence them by speaking through your work, delivering tangible results that expose their lack of substance.

### 6. The Patient Usurper
**Principles**: Widen Your Vision + Deep Observation + Suffer Fools Gladly
A rival patiently observes your habits and blind spots, keeping their eyes on a long-term goal, then strikes months later.
**Counter**: Widen your own vision and constantly evaluate how every action connects to your ultimate objectives.

### 7. The Weaponized Failure
**Principles**: Apprentice Yourself in Failure + The Seven Deadly Realities (Passive Aggression) + See Yourself as Others See You
A colleague intentionally lets a shared initiative fail, then claims noble "responsibility" while shifting blame to your department.
**Counter**: Dig into the exact mechanical details of the failure to expose their deliberate obstruction.

### 8. The Disruption Hijack
**Principles**: The Evolutionary Hijack + The Seven Deadly Realities (Flightiness) + Revert to a Feeling of Inferiority
A manager constantly changes direction, claiming they're "just experimenting" to mask their lack of discipline.
**Counter**: Focus on their track record of completed projects. Demand working prototypes before authorizing further pivots.

### 9. The Silo King
**Principles**: Occupy the Perfect Niche + The Seven Deadly Realities (Self-obsessiveness and Conformism)
An employee stakes out a specialized corner where no one else understands the procedures, creating an information monopoly.
**Counter**: Synthesize all forms of knowledge by learning the mechanics of their niche to demystify it.

### 10. The Ego Feeder
**Principles**: Suffer Fools Gladly + Submit to the Other + The Seven Deadly Realities (Envy)
Someone studies your insecurities and feeds your ego with excessive praise to extract concessions.
**Counter**: Recognize the vanity being exploited. Excessive upfront flattery is often disguised manipulation.

## Situation Templates

### 1. Turf War Over Software Rollout
- **Situation:** Operations resists your AI automation rollout.
- **Principles:** Deep Observation, Synthesize All Forms of Knowledge
- **Immediate:** Stop arguing; quietly observe the unwritten rules within operations.
- **Strategic:** Map a synthesized workflow bridging your sales objectives with their operational limitations.
- **Prevention:** Understand peer department pressures before asserting your initiatives.

### 2. Peer Undermines Your Initiative
- **Situation:** A peer executive subtly undermines your AI initiative in a leadership meeting.
- **Principles:** The Seven Deadly Realities, Speak Through Your Work
- **Immediate:** Recognize predictable envy; maintain a composed persona.
- **Strategic:** Deploy a pilot and gather undeniable revenue data.
- **Prevention:** Keep ideas guarded and prove concepts with data first.

### 3. Major Corporate Reorg
- **Situation:** A reorg changes reporting lines and alters your team's objectives.
- **Principles:** Let Go of the Past, Widen Your Vision
- **Immediate:** Abandon past identity and legacy processes without complaining.
- **Strategic:** Pivot your unique blend of skills to serve the new goals.
- **Prevention:** Continuously expand your skill base beyond your immediate job description.

### 4. Budget Slash Threat
- **Situation:** Finance attempts to slash your training and automation budget.
- **Principles:** Suffer Fools Gladly, The Seven Deadly Realities
- **Immediate:** Flatter finance executives by validating their cost-control mandate.
- **Strategic:** Frame your budget defense around their self-interest with cost-reduction data.
- **Prevention:** Preemptively send Finance regular cost-savings reports before budget season.

### 5. Rigid Vendor Refusal
- **Situation:** A vendor refuses to customize CRM features your reps need.
- **Principles:** Submit to the Other, Mechanical Intelligence
- **Immediate:** Step into the vendor's perspective to understand their pressures.
- **Strategic:** Build a hands-on workaround yourself to prove feasibility.
- **Prevention:** Personally test software before locking into long-term contracts.

### 6. Team Resists New Tool
- **Situation:** Your sales team resists adopting a new automated quoting tool you built.
- **Principles:** The Evolutionary Hijack, Alternate the Mind through "The Current"
- **Immediate:** Release as a fluid prototype and observe how reps actually interact with it.
- **Strategic:** Exploit unexpected usage patterns to redesign the software.
- **Prevention:** Never design from a whiteboard; continuously cycle between theory and feedback.

### 7. Egotistical Top Performer
- **Situation:** A brilliant rep acts egotistically and ignores the new playbook.
- **Principles:** Create a Back-and-Forth Dynamic, Suffer Fools Gladly
- **Immediate:** Accept their ego as a permanent fixture; don't engage in top-down lectures.
- **Strategic:** Challenge them to adapt the playbook to their unique style.
- **Prevention:** Allow high performers space to transfigure your ideas into their own voice.

### 8. Cross-Departmental Campaign Failure
- **Situation:** A major cross-departmental campaign misses its quarterly target.
- **Principles:** Apprentice Yourself in Failure, See Yourself as Others See You
- **Immediate:** View the miss objectively as a data point revealing structural flaws.
- **Strategic:** Analyze your own communication style for where bluntness alienated peers.
- **Prevention:** Launch small-scale tests to identify flaws before committing massive resources.

### 9. New Hires Overwhelmed
- **Situation:** New sales hires are overwhelmed by the complex product catalog and tech stack.
- **Principles:** Skills Acquisition, Transform Yourself Through Practice
- **Immediate:** Stop teaching high-level strategy; isolate one single fundamental task.
- **Strategic:** Drill relentlessly on that one fundamental until it becomes automatic.
- **Prevention:** Structure onboarding so reps master foundational mechanics through repetition.

### 10. CEO Issues Bad Mandate
- **Situation:** The CEO hands down a mandate that makes no sense for field realities.
- **Principles:** Deep Observation, Speak Through Your Work
- **Immediate:** Do not loudly rebel in the executive meeting.
- **Strategic:** Outwardly comply while quietly building a system that achieves the CEO's goal without following their flawed procedures.
- **Prevention:** Consistently deliver undeniable results to preempt executive interference.

## Weekly Mastery Audit (12 Questions)

1. Did I mistake someone's friendly mask for genuine support? (Seven Deadly Realities)
2. Did my professional persona strategically serve my goals, or did I default to raw openness? (Craft the Persona)
3. What unwritten rules did I observe before asserting myself? (Deep Observation)
4. Did I understand both the mechanics and the vision of what I worked on? (Combine How and What)
5. What knowledge from outside my discipline did I apply? (Synthesize Knowledge)
6. Did I lecture, or did I create an interactive dynamic? (Back-and-Forth)
7. What unexpected feedback did I incorporate rather than ignore? (Evolutionary Hijack)
8. Did I judge someone's actions from their perspective, or mine? (Submit to the Other)
9. What uncomfortable skill did I deliberately practice? (Move Toward Resistance)
10. Did I push through frustration or retreat to comfort? (Trust the Process)
11. Did I argue about an idea or prove it through execution? (Speak Through Work)
12. Did my decisions serve long-term goals or short-term fires? (Widen Your Vision)

## Growth Assessment Framework (9 Questions)

1. What Phase am I in for my coding skills: Apprenticeship, Creative, or Mastery? (Phase Model)
2. What Phase am I in for my leadership skills? (Phase Model)
3. What fundamental skill am I currently drilling to automaticity? (Practice Mode)
4. Who is my current mentor, and am I ready to challenge their methods? (Mentor Dynamic)
5. What am I avoiding because it's uncomfortable? (Resistance and Pain)
6. Am I building from a whiteboard or from hands-on experience? (Mechanical Intelligence)
7. What identity from my past am I still clinging to? (Let Go of the Past)
8. Where have I synthesized knowledge across disciplines this week? (Universal Man)
9. Am I testing hypotheses against reality, or theorizing in isolation? (The Current)

## Ethical Boundaries

### Craft the Appropriate Persona
- **Strategic**: Adjusting your communication style for different audiences while maintaining honest intent
- **Manipulative**: Creating a false identity to deceive colleagues about your qualifications or motives

### Suffer Fools Gladly
- **Strategic**: Accepting ego-driven behavior as predictable and working around it
- **Manipulative**: Weaponizing someone's insecurities by feeding their ego to extract unfair concessions

### Deep Observation
- **Strategic**: Studying power dynamics to tailor your approach and increase adoption
- **Manipulative**: Gathering personal vulnerabilities specifically for leverage or blackmail

### Speak Through Your Work
- **Strategic**: Letting results speak for themselves rather than engaging in political arguments
- **Manipulative**: Deliberately withholding communication to sabotage a peer's project

### The Seven Deadly Realities
- **Strategic**: Recognizing human flaws to protect yourself and frame requests around self-interest
- **Manipulative**: Deliberately triggering someone's envy or insecurity to destabilize them
